About

Baixin Guo (Max)

郭佰昕· /ˈbaɪ.ʃiːn ˈɡwoʊ/ · bye-sheen gwoh

I am an independent researcher studying how people calibrate trust in AI systems and how AI can communicate uncertainty more effectively. My work sits at the intersection of human-computer interaction, machine learning, and cognitive science.

Research Philosophy

My work builds a five-layer research stack on Precision Illusion: from defining the problem, to measuring it, to intervening, to personalizing, to making it reproducible across labs.

01 Theory

Taxonomizing how AI systems misrepresent uncertainty — pseudo-precise numerical claims, unwarranted overconfidence, and the cognitive mechanisms that make them persuasive.

02 Measurement

Build benchmarks and evaluation frameworks to characterize how LLMs express uncertainty (MaxFitCalib-Bench). Discovered the precision illusion — 6.3% of LLM outputs are pseudo-precise, citing numbers without evidence.

03 Intervention

Design and build automated pipelines that detect, diagnose, and correct miscalibrated outputs (CheckMyCoach). Improves overconfident outputs by +0.75 UCS at ~$0.001/correction.

04 Adaptation

Develop adaptive policies that select the right calibration intervention for each user and context (CalTrust). A LinUCB contextual bandit trained on 19,200 simulation trials.

05 Infrastructure

Build structured knowledge bases from domain textbooks (Knowledge Compiler) and a versioned schema + experiment platform so trust calibration data is mergeable across labs (InteractionKit).

Trust calibration research should produce deployable systems, not just papers. Every project on this site includes full reproducibility instructions — environment specs, quick-start guides, and CI-tested code.

Research Program

Core question: How do AI systems mislead through false precision, and how can we measure, fix, and prevent it?

I have produced: (1) a systematic taxonomy of how LLMs misrepresent uncertainty (Precision Illusion); (2) a benchmark and measurement methodology that detects the precision illusion where standard accuracy metrics cannot (MaxFitCalib-Bench); (3) a deployable correction pipeline that fixes miscalibrated outputs at ~$0.001/correction (CheckMyCoach); (4) an adaptive intervention framework that personalises calibration strategies per user (CalTrust); and (5) research infrastructure that makes trust calibration experiments reproducible and mergeable across labs (Knowledge Compiler, InteractionKit).

I am seeking a PhD position (Fall 2027) in a lab working on Human-AI Interaction, CSCW, or HCI + AI reliability. I bring a demonstrated ability to produce both theoretical contributions and deployable systems, and I am looking for an environment where I can extend the precision illusion framework to multi-domain settings (medicine, law) and test user-facing calibration interventions in controlled experiments.

Specific questions I want to pursue in a PhD: (a) Does the precision illusion generalise across domains with different stakes and user populations? (b) What interaction designs most effectively shorten the feedback loop between AI output and user trust calibration? (c) Can we build evaluation standards for AI uncertainty communication that the HCI community adopts?

System Building

All ML work runs CPU-only or via API — no GPU dependency. Tools: Python (scikit-learn, XGBoost, pingouin), R (lme4, lmerTest), Astro (this site), Streamlit, Docker.

Why HCI?

I am drawn to HCI because it demands both rigor and impact. A calibration metric that only an ML engineer can interpret is incomplete — it needs to connect to how users actually experience AI advice. Conversely, a user study that treats the AI output as a fixed stimulus misses the opportunity to improve the technology itself. HCI is the discipline that bridges these worlds, and that is where I want to contribute.
