The Precision Illusion: Why 6.3% Matters

In our recent benchmark of LLM fitness advice, we found something surprising: 0% overconfident outputs. The models weren't dangerously overconfident. They were subtly misleading.

6.3% of outputs contained precise-sounding numerical claims — "a 38.7% reduction" — without any evidence grounding. We call this the precision illusion.

Why does this matter? Because precise numbers carry disproportionate weight in human judgment. Zhang & Schwarz (2012) showed that people trust precise numbers more, even when they know the precision is unwarranted. A pseudo-precise output exploits this cognitive bias silently.

At a 6.3% rate per output, a user session involving 10 advice statements carries a ~48% cumulative probability of encountering at least one pseudo-precise response. And these outputs cluster in exactly the scenarios where users most need reliable advice — mixed-evidence and insufficient-evidence questions.
