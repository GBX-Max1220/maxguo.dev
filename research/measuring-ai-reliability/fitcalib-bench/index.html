MaxFitCalib-Bench

A Benchmark for LLM Calibration in Fitness Advice

The problem: LLMs are increasingly used for fitness advice, but how reliable are they? We built a 126-question benchmark grounded in ACSM/NSCA/AHA guidelines to find out.

The finding: 92.9% of outputs are calibrated, but 6.3% contain pseudo-precise claims — numbers that sound authoritative but lack evidence. This is the precision illusion, and it clusters in safety-critical scenarios.

Why this matters for HCI

Challenges the assumption that LLMs' primary risk is over-reliance — the bigger risk may be subtle misdirection through false precision that standard accuracy benchmarks cannot detect.

Methodology

We curated 126 fitness advice questions spanning 8 categories (nutrition, supplementation, training programming, injury prevention, etc.), each grounded in ACSM, NSCA, or AHA position stands. Questions were classified by evidence level and administered to frontier LLMs.

Outputs were evaluated using the UCS (Uncertainty Calibration Score) engine, which classifies each statement as Calibrated, Pseudo-precise, or Overconfident.

Key Takeaways

  1. 126 expert-crafted questions across 8 categories, grounded in ACSM/NSCA/AHA position stands.
  2. 92.9% of outputs Calibrated, but the dominant failure mode is Pseudo-precise (6.3%).
  3. Pseudo-precise outputs cluster in safety-critical scenarios: 23.7% on mixed-evidence, 26.7% on insufficient-evidence.
  4. ~48% cumulative exposure probability per 10-statement session.
